# Why this one matters

Freeman spent years inside Amazon writing six-pagers and after leaving published a reconstructed one for his own side-project, a game engine called Pixel Vision 8. What makes this sample uniquely useful is that it is *not* a PR/FAQ — it is the other public Amazon narrative form, the full six-page internal memo read at the start of a decision meeting. Sections are: Introduction, Goals, Tenets, State of the Business, Lessons Learned, Strategic Priorities, Appendix.

No published Amazon internal six-pager exists in the wild. Freeman's reconstruction is the closest public proxy.

Read it for three moves from `SKILL.md §5`:

- **Parallel-objective list** — the Tenets section reads as a numbered list of beliefs the team will operate from, each one-line, verb-or-noun-first, and shareable as a decision filter ("if a decision doesn't serve this tenet, reject it"). The tenets structure is the Amazon-specific move; it doesn't appear in the PR/FAQ form but it is foundational to the narrative six-pager.
- **Objection inoculation** — the Lessons Learned section is an honest accounting of what has failed so far. By staging its own failures before the reader can, the memo buys credibility for the strategic priorities that follow.
- **Disintermediation setup, at small scale** — the Introduction and State of the Business frame why the hobby-tool category is shifting (market context, adjacent-tool consolidation) and position Pixel Vision 8's bet as the response.

# How to read it for craft

Freeman's narrative is the cleanest published example of the *non-PR-FAQ* six-pager — the form that gets used for in-flight business review, strategic reset, or major decision-point memo rather than for new-product launch. The PR/FAQ is for "should we build this?". The narrative six-pager is for "what should we do with what we've built?".

The tenets section is the one to study hardest. Amazon's discipline is that tenets are not values or principles — they are operational filters stable over a multi-year window. A well-written tenet tells you what to decide in a conflict; a poorly-written tenet reads like a values poster. Compare Freeman's tenets (specific, operationally sharp) to the generic "we value innovation" tenets that most companies publish. That is the bar.

Lessons Learned is the other distinctive move. Most strategic memos skip over past failures; the six-pager form forces them onto the page. The discipline here is that Lessons Learned should name at least one failure the author is still embarrassed about. If it only names "challenges we overcame," it is not Lessons Learned; it is marketing.

# How it maps to the `/six-pager` 9-section form

The narrative six-pager and the 9-section strategic memo described in `SKILL.md` are adjacent forms, not identical. A rough mapping:

| Freeman's sections | `/six-pager` 9-section form |
|---|---|
| Introduction | Background + Opportunity |
| Goals | Strategic Imperative (numbered objectives) |
| Tenets | (no direct equivalent — lives as subtext across voice rules) |
| State of the Business | Opportunity + [Proposer] Advantages |
| Lessons Learned | Risks and Mitigations (inverted — past risk materialized) |
| Strategic Priorities | Product Concept + Execution Plan |
| (implicit ask) | Conclusion |

Use the Freeman reference to calibrate voice and tenets discipline; use the 9-section form for institutional-exec audiences where the argument-for-funding structure is load-bearing.
