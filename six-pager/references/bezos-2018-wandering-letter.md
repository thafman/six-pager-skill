# Why this one matters

The 2018 letter takes a risk most strategic memos won't: it argues *against* efficiency as a universal value. The core move is holding two opposing framings — "efficiency" and "wandering" — simultaneously and refusing to collapse them. That is the defensive-AND-offensive pattern applied to a higher-level abstraction than usual, and it's a masterclass in the move.

Read it for three moves from `SKILL.md §5`:

- **Defensive-AND-offensive framing** — "wandering" is framed as *both* a tolerated inefficiency *and* a required capability for non-linear discovery. The letter refuses the either/or.
- **Term-of-art coinage** — *"wandering"* is used as a technical term for intuition-guided exploration, defined in a single clause and then scaffolded through the rest of the argument.
- **Future-dated endpoint, embedded** — the letter treats AWS SageMaker as the proof-point, arguing backward from a concrete launched product to the wandering that produced it. The endpoint (SageMaker shipped) anchors the case for the method (wandering works).

# How to read it for craft

This letter is the easiest one to steal a specific move from: the *paradox holder*. When the reader is expecting you to pick a side (efficiency vs. exploration, discipline vs. invention, hedge vs. bet), refuse. State both framings in the same sentence. Give each its due. Then argue that the discipline is to hold both at once.

The letter also embeds a good example of backing a claim with a specific product history. AWS SageMaker is named, the years of wandering that led to it are summarized, and the summary is used to justify the general principle. Observe the direction: not "here is our principle, let me justify it with examples" but "here is a product you already know shipped; here is what produced it; here is the principle." The evidence arrives before the claim lands.

# Short excerpt illustrating the paradox-holder move

From the section on wandering (paraphrased for brevity; full text at source):

> Wandering in business is not efficient ... but it's also not random. It's guided — by hunch, by gut, by intuition, by curiosity, and powered by a deep conviction that the prize for customers is big enough that it's worth being a little messy and tangential to find our way there.

Notice how the sentence admits the cost (not efficient) in the same breath as it defends the value (guided, not random, conviction-driven). That structure is worth memorizing. When you're about to defend a position that sounds indefensible, try admitting the cost first — it buys the claim that follows.
