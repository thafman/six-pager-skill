# References — public Amazon-style strategic writing

## What's in here

A library of public strategic writing that sits in the same tradition as Amazon's internal six-pager — the narrative memo that replaced PowerPoint at S-team meetings. Each file opens with a header noting the author, year, source URL, and the rhetorical moves from `SKILL.md §5` it exemplifies.

Use these as calibration material. Read one or two before drafting a new memo so your voice and moves are primed by the bar, not by whatever ChatGPT has last seen.

## The landscape (be honest about it)

Amazon's actual internal six-pagers are confidential by policy. Ones that leak get pulled down. The closest public corpus falls into four buckets:

**1. Shareholder letters** — Jeff Bezos's annual letters are narrative memos addressed to shareholders, published with the SEC, explicitly written in the same voice that inspired the internal six-pager ritual. These are the canonical public Amazon narrative writing.

**2. Authored samples from ex-Amazonians** — Ian McAllister (ex-Amazon director), Colin Bryar & Bill Carr (*Working Backwards* book authors), Jesse Freeman (ex-Amazon engineer). Each has published a constructed example to teach the form.

**3. Insider craft notes** — Brad Porter (former Amazon VP & Distinguished Engineer) has published the best public operating notes on *why* the form works — silent-reading ritual, narrative-coherence discipline, the press release as "paper model."

**4. External artifacts verified by Amazon insiders** — the Wilke/Bishop six-pager (below) was written by a journalist in preparation for an interview with then-Amazon Consumer CEO Jeff Wilke, reviewed by Wilke, and confirmed as faithful to the Amazon internal form. This is the closest thing to an authentic six-pager that exists in the public record.

**5. Real applied PR/FAQs from operators in the wild** — People outside Amazon who have actually shipped the method on their own products and made the artifact public.

A sixth adjacent category — antitrust-discovery excerpts (FTC v. Amazon, House Judiciary 2020 report) — surfaces phrases and fragments, but not whole memos. Worth knowing the phrases exist; not usable as structural exemplars.

## A note on licensing

Every reference here is either in the public record (SEC filings) or explicitly made public by its author (personal blog, LinkedIn post, Coda doc, GeekWire CDN, book-excerpt page). Where excerpt length risks overstepping, the file links out and paraphrases instead. Full-text reproductions credit the source URL in the header.
