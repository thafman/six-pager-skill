# Why this one matters

This is the closest thing to an authentic internal Amazon six-pager that exists in the public record. Todd Bishop, editor of GeekWire, wrote a full six-pager for his 2017 on-stage interview with Jeff Wilke — then Amazon's Worldwide Consumer CEO — and circulated it to Wilke and the audience in advance. Wilke read it, confirmed the form adhered to Amazon's internal practice, and let it stand as the canonical framing for the session. The PDF is still hosted on the GeekWire CDN.

Every other reference in the library is a teaching sample, a reconstruction, or a derivative PR/FAQ. This one has been reviewed by a sitting Amazon CEO and deemed faithful to the actual internal form. Read it first if you read nothing else.

# What's in it (structurally)

Six pages exactly, in four parts:

1. **Fictional press release** (page 1) — written as if the GeekWire Summit interview had already happened. Dated, quoted, "About GeekWire / About Amazon" boilerplate included. This mirrors Amazon's internal convention: start with the end-state, as if the event has landed.
2. **Narrative sections** (pages 2–4) — three titled blocks:
   - *Jeff Wilke's Personal Background and Influences*
   - *Amazon's Business, Culture and Future*
   - *The Future of Business and Technology*
   - Each opens with two paragraphs of grounded context (stats, named products, dated milestones) and closes with a *Related Topics/Questions* bulleted list. The questions are the active content — the paragraphs exist to ground them.
3. **FAQ** (page 5) — five Q&A pairs covering the meta-questions a reader would bring: what is this doc, why was it made, will the session follow it exactly, what happens with feedback. Exactly the "internal FAQ" discipline Amazon's own PR/FAQs use, but applied to a journalist's preparation artifact.
4. **Appendix / Endnotes** (page 6) — ten numbered citations supporting specific claims in the narrative. SEC filings, prior articles, videos, annual reports. Each endnote is linked to a specific superscripted reference in the body.

Total length is around 2,000 words — almost exactly the six-page target at Amazon's three-minutes-per-page silent-read pace.

# Moves it exemplifies

- **Future-dated endpoint.** The press release is dated for an event that hasn't happened yet. Same move as McAllister's Circulert, but applied to a conversation rather than a product.
- **Disintermediation setup.** The Business section frames Amazon's growth against the backdrop of e-commerce displacing brick-and-mortar, then positions Whole Foods as the acquisition that runs the displacement in reverse.
- **Objection inoculation** — used implicitly throughout via the FAQ. Questions the reader is about to ask (*will this be scripted? why did you make this?*) are answered before they form.
- **Every claim grounded.** The narrative paragraphs name specific numbers — $1.6B net sales in 1999, $136B in 2016, 5,000 employees to 380,000, $13.7B Whole Foods deal, 80M Prime members. Every load-bearing assertion has a stat, a named company, or a cited source in the appendix.

# What to steal from it for your own memos

**The Appendix is a first-class section, not an afterthought.** Numbered endnotes in the body, corresponding entries in the Appendix, with URLs to primary sources. This is how an exec memo shows its work without cluttering the narrative. The Wilke memo has ten endnotes in six pages — roughly one per page of narrative.

**The press release is pre-FAQ framing, not marketing copy.** The page-one PR here is written to set the stage for *what happened* at the interview, not to sell anyone on the interview. The tone is declarative, stat-grounded, mildly celebratory in exactly the way a retrospective write-up would be. If your own PR reads like ad copy, rewrite it as a retrospective.

**Narrative coherence across pages.** Read this memo straight through and watch how a claim in page 2 (Wilke's operational background) sets up a question in page 3 (how that background shapes Amazon's end-to-end system) which is answered in page 4 (the fulfillment + autonomous + Prime triangle). This is the "page 2 question, page 4 answer" discipline that Brad Porter attributes to Bezos — see `porter-6-pager-notes.md`.
