# Why this one matters

Bryar and Carr spent a combined twenty-seven years at Amazon, including as members of Jeff Bezos's shadow program and as leaders of Amazon Studios, Kindle, and other products that were born as PR/FAQs. The Melinda sample is their own construction — a hypothetical smart-mailbox product they used to teach the form in their book *Working Backwards* and the companion Coda doc. Of all the published samples, this one carries the most authority because the authors are the people the rest of the ecosystem learned the method from.

Read it for two moves from `SKILL.md §5`:

- **Future-dated endpoint** — the PR opens with a concrete launch date, a $299 price, a named customer (Janet Thomas), named distribution channels, and a specific spec sheet (camera, scale, fingerprint reader, two-inch insulated walls, twelve hours of cooling). The bet is made tangible enough to be falsifiable.
- **Objection inoculation** — the Melinda sample pairs the PR with an external FAQ that pre-answers obvious skeptical questions ("isn't this just a cooler?", "what about theft?", "will the courier actually know how to use it?"). That structure is the PR/FAQ's core contribution: it forces the author to stage the doubts before the reader does.

# How to read it for craft

Melinda is useful precisely because the product is mundane. It is not a flashy consumer AI bet; it is a mailbox. The discipline the sample teaches is that the form works on mundane bets too — maybe especially on mundane bets, where the temptation to over-sell is strongest. The PR stays plain, the spec is concrete, and the customer quote sounds like something a customer would actually say in an interview.

The other craft lesson is about quote construction. The leader quote in a PR/FAQ is not a marketing statement; it is a thesis sentence. It should say *why* the company is building this, not *what* the product does. When you draft your own, test the leader quote by deleting it and asking whether the PR still makes the same argument. If yes, the quote was decoration; rewrite it.

# Short excerpt illustrating the future-dated-endpoint move

From the Melinda sample press release (paraphrased; full text at source):

> Blue Corp. today announced Melinda, a smart mailbox that ensures secure and properly chilled delivery and storage for your online purchases and groceries. Melinda is available today at $299 at keepitcoolmelinda.com and through Amazon, Walmart, and select partners.

Notice the concreteness: the name of the proposing company, the product name, the specific price point, the specific distribution channels, and the specific promise (secure, chilled, online purchases *and* groceries). Nothing is hedged. The PR does not say "we hope to bring to market"; it says "available today at $299." That commitment is the forcing function — the team cannot write this sentence unless they believe the product will ship.
