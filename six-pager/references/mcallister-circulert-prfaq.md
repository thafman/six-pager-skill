# Why this one matters

McAllister spent years at Amazon running the Working Backwards process and eventually shipped a public version of the PR template with a worked example. The example, *Circulert*, is a fictional consumer-facing notification app — the kind of product where the PR has to sell a vision without over-promising features. It's the single most-cited public example of the Working Backwards press release form and it's what teams outside Amazon usually learn from first.

Read it for the canonical move from `SKILL.md §5`:

- **Future-dated endpoint** — the entire PR is written as if the product launched today. Everything — the dateline, the problems named, the customer quote, the "visit X.com to start" footer — is present-tense. The move is to force the author to describe a world where the bet already landed. That constraint does most of the analytical work.

# The template structure

McAllister's template has a predictable skeleton, repeated across every PR/FAQ that followed it:

1. **Title** — `[Company] announces [product] to enable [customer] to [benefit]`
2. **Subtitle** — one sentence reframing the announcement with additional detail
3. **Dateline** — `[City, State] — [Intended launch date]`
4. **Intro paragraph** — three to four crisp sentences expanding on the title
5. **Problem** — the top three or four customer pains, ranked by severity
6. **Solution** — how the product addresses each named problem
7. **Leader quote** — the company's why, in first person, from an exec
8. **How it works** — a plain-language walk-through of the customer experience
9. **Customer quote** — a fictional quote that sounds like a real quote
10. **Getting started** — a specific URL or first step

The discipline is to fit the whole thing on one page. If a section balloons, the PR has told the author something: the underlying idea is not sharp enough yet.

# How to read it for craft

Two things to notice about the Circulert example specifically.

First, the problem section names four pains ranked by severity, not an abstract "we've all been there" setup. Each pain is a sentence with a scenario and a consequence. The move is to force the reader to recognize their own experience before the product is introduced. When you draft your own PR, try writing the problems as one-line scenarios before drafting anything else; if they don't ring true, the product doesn't have a customer yet.

Second, the customer quote is carefully mundane. It does not sound aspirational; it sounds like something a tired shopper would actually say. That mundanity is the quality bar — quotes that sound like marketing copy are the tell of a PR that hasn't been written backwards from a real user.

# Where to pair this with a `/six-pager`

In the DRAFT flow (working-backwards sub-mode), the PR is the first artifact. This reference is the voice and structural target for that PR. After the PR is approved, the 9-section memo is drafted around it: the PR anchors Product Concept, the Execution Plan phases work backward to the PR date, and the Conclusion ask funds the plan.
