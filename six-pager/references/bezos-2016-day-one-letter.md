# Why this one matters

Three coined terms in a single letter, each now institutional shorthand inside and outside Amazon: *Day 1*, *high-velocity decision-making*, and *disagree and commit*. Study this one for term-of-art coinage specifically — the move where the author names a concept, defines it in the same sentence, and then uses the name as load-bearing structure for the rest of the argument.

Read it for four moves from `SKILL.md §5`:

- **Term-of-art coinage (three times over)** — each concept is named, defined, and then re-deployed as a structural element.
- **Objection inoculation** — the letter opens by anticipating the reader's obvious question ("How do you stay Day 1 at a company Amazon's size?") and answers it as the organizing frame.
- **Parallel-objective list** — the four antidotes to Day 2 (customer obsession, resist proxies, embrace external trends, high-velocity decisions) are structured as a numbered menu, each with verb-first prose.
- **Future-dated endpoint, implicit** — the letter frames the "always Day 1" stance as the commitment that binds future decision-making. The reader is asked to judge future Bezos letters against it.

# How to read it for craft

Watch how the letter sequences abstraction and specificity. Each new term-of-art is immediately cashed out in an operating detail. *High-velocity decisions* is defined, then paired with the "70% of the information" threshold. *Disagree and commit* is defined, then illustrated by a specific episode where the author overrode his own opinion to commit to a team's conviction. Abstraction → concrete mechanism → example. That three-beat rhythm is what keeps the letter from drifting into aphorism.

The objection-inoculation opening is worth lifting wholesale as a structural move for your own memos. It works because it states the reader's skepticism more forcefully than the reader would state it, and answers by changing the frame rather than defending.

# Short excerpt illustrating term-of-art coinage

On the Day 2 framing (paraphrased for brevity; full text at source):

> Day 2 is stasis. Followed by irrelevance. Followed by excruciating, painful decline. Followed by death. And that is why it is always Day 1.

Notice the cascade structure — each word raises the stakes one notch. By the end, "Day 2" has been defined not as a state but as a trajectory. That definition does all the subsequent work in the letter; every antidote is framed as a way to not-slide into the cascade.
